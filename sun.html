The Sun

Introduction

The Sun is the star at the center of the Solar System. It provides the energy that sustains life on Earth and influences the climate and weather. The Sun's immense gravitational pull keeps the Solar System's planets in orbit.

Key Facts

  • Diameter: 1.39 million kilometers
  • Surface Temperature: Approximately 5,500°C
  • Core Temperature: Approximately 15 million°C
  • Distance from Earth: About 149.6 million kilometers
  • Composition: About 74% hydrogen, 24% helium, and 2% other elements

Structure

The Sun consists of several layers, each playing a vital role in its overall function:

  • Core: The central region where nuclear fusion occurs.
  • Radiative Zone: Energy slowly moves outward through radiation.
  • Convective Zone: Hot plasma rises and cooler plasma sinks, creating convection currents.
  • Photosphere: The visible surface of the Sun.
  • Chromosphere: A layer above the photosphere emitting a reddish glow.
  • Corona: The outermost layer, extending millions of kilometers into space.

Energy Production

The Sun generates energy through nuclear fusion, a process in which hydrogen nuclei combine to form helium. This process releases an immense amount of energy, which reaches Earth as light and heat.

Solar Activity

The Sun exhibits dynamic phenomena that influence space weather:

  • Sunspots: Darker, cooler areas on the Sun's surface caused by magnetic activity.
  • Solar Flares: Sudden eruptions of energy that release radiation across the electromagnetic spectrum.
  • Coronal Mass Ejections (CMEs): Massive bursts of solar wind and magnetic fields ejected into space.

These events can impact satellite operations, communication systems, and power grids on Earth.

Did You Know?

The Sun is about 4.6 billion years old and is expected to continue shining for another 5 billion years before transitioning into a red giant and eventually becoming a white dwarf.
